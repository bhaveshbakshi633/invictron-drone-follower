# Analysis

A ROS2 Humble stack in which a PX4 SITL quadrotor (Gazebo) follows a simulated car driving a figure-8. The `follower` node consumes `/car/position` and publishes `/drone/waypoint` at 50 Hz — a point 5 m behind the car at 20 m altitude — with car-position timeout (>200 ms -> hover), single-step jump rejection (>5 m -> hold last valid), and all thresholds in `config/params.yaml`. `px4_interface` arms, takes off, and streams OFFBOARD setpoints at 20 Hz over uXRCE-DDS with ENU->NED conversion; `health_monitor` watches RTF; events.log + telemetry.jsonl plus `log_summary.py`/`plot_run.py` and a headless Docker CI run round it out.

**Completion status:** Core follow loop, failure handling (timeout/jump/arm-retry), logging, tooling, and CI are complete; full end-to-end PX4 SITL run validated headless in Docker for 60 s. The car is a scripted `/car/position` source (`car_sim`), not a spawned Gazebo model — `car_viz` optionally renders a matching box in the GUI, but the follower reads only the topic. A nominal run trips none of the four failure paths; each is reproducible on demand (`scripts/demo_failures.sh`). The follow policy (jump gate + feed-forward waypoint) is unit-tested (`test/test_follow_policy.py`, run in CI); the log/gate/plot tools are CI-tested against `sample_logs/`.

## Q1 — The Conflict

The 150 ms latency / ±0.8 m / ~6–7 Hz camera and the control team's 50 ms cadence only conflict if you insist the *measurement* rate equal the *control* rate. It shouldn't. First questions to the AI team: is the 150 ms latency **fixed or jittery**, and is it a wall-clock delay I can timestamp at capture, or pipeline-variable? Is the ±0.8 m noise **white or biased** (calibration/extrinsics offset), and is it per-axis? What is the car's **max speed and acceleration** in this scene? Those three answers size the estimator: fixed latency with a capture timestamp means I forward-predict a deterministic 150 ms and the compensation is exact; jitter means I predict per-measurement to actual receipt time and must inflate process noise, so I'd push for a hardware capture timestamp rather than eat the variance. White noise justifies a light constant-velocity Kalman filter (or alpha-beta tracker) that averages it down; a *bias* can't be filtered and needs a calibration fix upstream, because forward-prediction over 150 ms would otherwise project the bias plus a velocity error into the setpoint. Car dynamics set the CV model's process-noise term and tell me whether "constant velocity" is even adequate on the figure-8 or whether I need a constant-turn-rate model.

To the control team: is 50 Hz a **hard requirement, or can your controller interpolate/extrapolate** its own reference between updates? If they truly need 50 Hz, the architecture is a CV Kalman filter running at 50 Hz that *predicts* between the 6–7 Hz measurement corrections and outputs a smooth, latency-compensated setpoint — the drone never sees the raw 6–7 Hz camera or its noise. That directly resolves the oscillation: oscillation comes from feeding stale, jumpy points into a fast loop, which the estimator eliminates by decoupling the two rates. One interaction to watch: my 5 m jump-reject threshold and the ±0.8 m noise now live in the same space. With σ=0.8 m, raw single-step deltas can approach ~2–3 m from noise alone at low update rates, so a fixed 5 m gate is crude — I'd replace it with a Mahalanobis/innovation gate (reject at ~3σ of the *predicted* innovation covariance) so genuine noise is smoothed while a real teleport is still caught, without a magic constant that fights the filter.

## Q2 — The Bug

Fix it and tell someone, with a one-line written record (commit message + a note in the shared channel/issue). Never silent: a wrong coordinate frame isn't a local defect, it corrupts everyone's *mental model* of the data. If I patch it quietly, the next engineer who reads the node, or anyone who built a compensating hack on top of the broken frame, re-derives the whole thing from scratch or has their workaround silently break. The written record is what makes the fix cheap for everyone else — the fix is 10 minutes, the shared understanding is the actual deliverable. Silent fixes also destroy the audit trail when someone later asks "when did the frame change and why did results shift."

Two hours before a demo the *calculus* changes, not the honesty. Now it's risk-of-regression versus reward, and I assess blast radius first: who consumes this topic, and does the demo path actually exercise the bug? If the demo currently "works" because downstream code was tuned around the broken frame, dropping in an untested correct-frame change two hours out can cascade and is the higher-risk move. So: prefer a reversible, flagged fix (parameter or launch-arg gated, defaulting to current behavior) that I can validate and flip, or — if I can't test it in time — document it as a known issue with the exact workaround and ship the demo on the known-good build, then fix properly after. What I don't do either way is push an unvalidated change into a live demo or stay quiet. Be decisive: correct-frame behind a flag if testable, documented known-issue if not.

## Q3 — Your Weaknesses

**(1) No real state estimator — the follower acts on raw last-valid position.** The 5 m jump-reject + 200 ms hover logic is a pair of hard thresholds, not an estimator, so it degrades badly under realistic sensor input. Under the Q1 camera (±0.8 m, ~6–7 Hz), single-step deltas from noise alone reach ~2–3 m, so the drone's setpoint jitters ±0.8 m at 20 m altitude with no smoothing; drop the update rate and the 200 ms timeout starts tripping into hover on every third or fourth frame, producing visible stutter. It will chatter under noise and lag under latency, and the fixed 5 m gate can't distinguish a big noise spike from a real jump. Against ground truth today this looks clean because `car_sim` noise is off by default (`noise_stddev_m=0.0`); turn on real noise and it breaks predictably here first.

**(2) Open-loop geometric follow — corner-cutting; the tracking lag is fixed at the root.** The waypoint is placed `offset` behind the car's reported position along its heading. Measured on the figure-8 with the original position-only setpoints (velocity `NaN`): the drone trailed **~10 m mean, ~20 m peak** — the intended 5 m offset *plus* ~5 m of lag from PX4's position controller chasing a moving target — and overshot the car's path envelope (drone Y-extent **±12 m vs the car's ±10 m**). Root cause was the position-only setpoint; the fix is `px4_interface` now sending the setpoint's **velocity feed-forward** (finite-diff of the waypoint stream) so the controller leads the moving target rather than trailing it — the run's scorecard reports the resulting follow error. What remains is **cross-track** error on the tight lobes — no path-curvature model or pure-pursuit — which grows with car speed. That is the tracking limitation to expect breaking first under a faster or sharper path. (Earlier drafts under-stated this at "0.5–1.5 m"; the real baseline figure is the ~5 m lag above.) **(3) PX4 OFFBOARD robustness is simulation-thin.** The 20 Hz setpoint stream has no watchdog/failsafe if the uXRCE-DDS link stalls (PX4 drops OFFBOARD after ~500 ms of missed setpoints and there's no defined recovery), the arm-to-takeoff-to-offboard handoff has a timing race that a retry count masks rather than fixes, and everything is validated only in sim — no wind, no GPS loss, no EKF divergence. It's also RTF-sensitive: under software rendering the health monitor warns below 0.8 RTF, and at low RTF the real-vs-sim time skew can itself starve the setpoint cadence.

## Q4 — One More Week

Replace the raw last-valid + fixed-threshold heuristic with a proper constant-velocity Kalman filter with innovation-based outlier gating, running at the 50 Hz control rate off the measurement stream. This is the single highest-leverage change because it attacks the *root cause* shared by weaknesses (1), (2), and (3) at once: it smooths the ±0.8 m noise (fixing chatter), forward-predicts through the 150 ms latency and across dropouts (fixing lag and the hover-stutter on missed frames), and yields a velocity estimate that feeds directly into pursuit/feedforward and into a smoother, better-timed setpoint stream. The 5 m jump-reject and 200 ms timeout collapse into one principled mechanism — gate on the predicted innovation covariance, coast on the model during short gaps — instead of two brittle magic numbers.

It beats the alternatives cleanly. Tuning controller gains or the existing thresholds only trades one failure mode for another (smoother but laggier, or snappier but chattier) because the information problem — noisy, delayed, intermittent measurements — is untouched. Adding features (better pursuit geometry, more failsafes) is real work but each fixes only its own symptom, and pursuit built on an unfiltered position estimate just tracks noise more aggressively. The Kalman filter is the piece that makes every other improvement land: it's a few hundred lines, testable in isolation with replayed telemetry.jsonl, and it converts the system from "geometric waypoint placer that happens to work in clean sim" into something that degrades gracefully under real sensor data — which is exactly what the "we'll test what breaks" evaluation is looking for.
